"Aristotle on Learning to Be Good"

Moral education is fundamentally about learning to take pleasure in the right things. Habituation shapes emotions before reason can operate. The well-brought-up person possesses "the that" — a reliable sense of what is noble — which is the prerequisite for philosophical understanding ("the because").

Core Argument You cannot reason your way into virtue from scratch. Correct emotional formation through habituation must come first; philosophical understanding builds on top of it.
Cooper I

Reason and Human Good — Deliberation

Deliberation is not merely instrumental means-end reasoning. It includes specifying what the good life consists in. "Things toward ends" (ta pros ta telē) includes constituents of the end, not just tools to achieve it. Nous grasps both universal principles and particular facts.

Core Argument Deliberation has a broader scope than commonly assumed — it helps determine what happiness concretely involves, not merely how to get there.
Cooper II

Reason and Human Good — The Best Life

Negotiates the tension between the intellectualist reading (eudaimonia = contemplation) and the inclusivist reading (eudaimonia = full range of virtues). Reason plays a dual role as both the substance of contemplation and the guiding principle of practical life. Phronēsis functions architectonically.

Core Argument The ideal life gives primacy to contemplation but essentially includes practical virtues. Phronēsis organizes and orders all other goods.
Jiminez Ch. 2

Boulēsis — Rational Desire

Boulēsis (wish) is desire for the good that belongs to the rational part of the soul. For the virtuous, what appears good is genuinely good. The spoudaios (good person) serves as the measure of correct boulēsis. Unlike prohairesis, boulēsis can target impossible things.

Core Argument As character develops through habituation, boulēsis changes — one comes to wish for genuinely good things, which reshapes deliberation and action.
Jiminez Ch. 4

Phronēsis — Practical Wisdom

Practical wisdom requires both correct reasoning and correct desire — the reciprocity thesis. Distinguishes phronēsis from mere cleverness (deinotēs): cleverness is ethically neutral, phronēsis is cleverness plus correct orientation toward the good. Supports the unity of the virtues.

Core Argument Virtue of character and phronēsis co-develop gradually. Neither can exist without the other, and together they ground the unity of the virtues.
Jiminez Ch. 6

Deliberation and the Virtuous Agent

Against the view that the virtuous person acts "spontaneously," argues that virtuous action characteristically involves deliberation. Since prohairesis requires deliberation and virtue requires prohairesis, virtue requires deliberation. Deliberation is not only for difficult cases — it is how general commitments are fitted to particular situations.

Core Argument The good life involves ongoing deliberative engagement — the phronimos continuously assesses and responds to changing circumstances.

Key Tensions & Debates

Intellectualist Reading

Eudaimonia = contemplation (theōria). Practical virtue is secondary, valued mainly for enabling the contemplative life.

VS

Inclusivist Reading

Eudaimonia = complete virtue including both practical and theoretical. Cooper argues for primacy of contemplation within an inclusive framework.

Narrow Deliberation

We deliberate only about instrumental means to pre-given ends — a purely calculative view of practical reason.

VS

Broad Deliberation

Deliberation includes specifying what ends consist in and weighing competing goods. Cooper's and Jiminez's preferred reading.

Spontaneous Virtue

The truly virtuous person just "sees" what to do and acts immediately, without needing to deliberate.

VS

Deliberative Virtue

Virtuous action characteristically involves deliberation — fitting general commitments to particulars. Jiminez Ch. 6's argument.
